📖 Nutrition Data Sources

[2]USDA FoodData Central (2023) — U.S. Department of Agriculture, Agricultural Research Service. Used for international foods, vitamins, and cross-validation of micronutrient values. fdc.nal.usda.gov
[3]Mifflin-St Jeor Equation (1990) — Mifflin MD et al., "A new predictive equation for resting energy expenditure in healthy individuals." Am J Clin Nutr, 51(2):241-247. Used for BMR calculation.
[4]WHO/FAO Dietary Reference Values — Joint WHO/FAO Expert Consultation on Human Vitamin and Mineral Requirements (2004). Used for Vitamin D, B12, Folate, Zinc, and Magnesium targets.
[5]IFCT (Indian Food Composition Tables, 2017) — National Institute of Nutrition, Hyderabad. Detailed composition of traditional Indian foods including street foods and regional staples.
[6]NIH Office of Dietary Supplements — Fact Sheets for Health Professionals on Iron, Calcium, Vitamin C, Vitamin D, Vitamin B12, Folate, Zinc, Magnesium, Potassium. ods.od.nih.gov
ℹ️ About the Nutrient Calculations

Energy Targets — Calculated using the Mifflin-St Jeor BMR formula multiplied by an activity factor (1.2–1.725). Goal adjustments (−500/+300 kcal) are applied on top. This estimates TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure).

Macro Targets — Protein at 1.2g/kg body weight; Carbohydrates at 50% of energy / 4 kcal per gram; Fat at 30% of energy / 9 kcal per gram. These align with ICMR-NIN general population guidelines.

Micronutrient Targets — Age/gender-adjusted where practical (e.g., Iron: 8mg male, 18mg female; Calcium: 1000mg adult). Potassium 3500mg, Magnesium 320–420mg, Zinc 8–11mg, Folate 400mcg are WHO/NIH standard adult values.

Food Composition — Values represent approximate averages per typical serving. Actual values vary by cooking method, variety, freshness, and region. Raw vs cooked differences are not modelled in this version.

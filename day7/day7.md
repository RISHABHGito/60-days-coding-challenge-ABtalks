
What I learned today: The quality of your AI output is almost entirely determined by the quality of your input.
Most people treat AI like a search engine — type a few words and hope for the best. I used to do the same. But a recent deep 
dive into prompt engineering completely changed how I think about working with large language models.

Here are the key insights I took away:

1 Context is everything. Giving Claude your role, your goal, and your constraints produces dramatically sharper
output than a bare question. The model can only work with what you give it.
2 Structure your prompt like a brief. Specifying the format, depth, and audience of the response — before asking 
the question — eliminates most back-and-forth iterations.
3 Effort settings change the game. Matching the model's reasoning depth to task complexity (standard for routine
work, high for tradeoff-heavy problems) keeps quality high without wasting time.
4 Prompting is a transferable skill. The same principles — clarity, specificity, structured thinking — that make a
good prompt make a good engineering spec, a good interview answer, and a good grant proposal.
5 AI is a thinking partner, not a shortcut. The best results come when you bring your own reasoning first, and use
the model to stress-test, extend, or articulate it — not replace it.

My next steps: applying these principles to my ongoing project — Kisaan Saathi, an AI-assisted agri-tech tool for 
Indian farmers — and building a personal prompt library for DSA, system design prep, and technical writing.

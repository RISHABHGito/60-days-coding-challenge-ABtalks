🔍 **Today's learning: your app list quietly tells a story about you — no hacking required.**

I spent today digging into how a "digital footprint" can be reconstructed from something as ordinary as the 
list of apps on someone's phone. No passwords, no private data — just public facts about which platforms collect what.

The big idea: an app list like Instagram, WhatsApp, Spotify, Amazon, and Google Pay isn't just "things I use.
" It's a signal. Map each app to its parent company, and patterns appear fast — how concentrated your data is 
in one ecosystem (Google and Meta own more of our digital lives than most of us realize), how your exposure 
spreads across social, shopping, communication, and entertainment, and where the truly high-value data lives: location, spending habits, social graph.

The more important lesson, though, was about discipline in inference. "I use Spotify" is a fact. "I'm probably a
young, entertainment-heavy consumer" is an estimate — and it should always be labeled as one. Good privacy analysis
never blurs that line and never claims certainty it doesn't have.

Prototyping a mini privacy dashboard today made it click: most of what feels like surveillance isn't secret access.
It's smart aggregation of things we already chose to share.
